# Medical Necessity Guidelines for Appeals

## Definition
- **Medical necessity**: A service is medically necessary when it is appropriate and needed for the diagnosis or treatment of the patient’s condition, and meets accepted standards of care. Payers often deny when documentation does not clearly support need for the service, level of service, or frequency.

## What to include in an appeal
- **Clinical indication**: Why the service was needed for this patient at this time (symptoms, diagnosis, risk).
- **Relevant history and exam**: Summary from the record that supports the decision.
- **Treatment plan**: How the service fits into the overall plan (e.g., response to prior treatment, change in condition).
- **Guideline reference**: When applicable, reference the payer’s medical policy, LCD, NCD, or evidence-based guideline and show that the patient meets the criteria.

## Common denial codes and approach
- **CO50 / M51 (medical necessity, missing medical record)**: Submit progress notes, treatment plan, and any lab/imaging that support necessity.
- **CO97 / N362 (service not covered with this diagnosis, diagnosis policy conflict)**: Provide clinical documentation that ties the diagnosis to the service and, if applicable, cite the coverage policy that allows the combination.
- **CO167 / N382 (diagnosis not covered, policy diagnosis restriction)**: Document how the diagnosis is supported and how it meets the payer’s coverage policy.

## Documentation best practices
- Link each billed service to a diagnosis and to the note that documents the encounter.
- For repeated services (e.g., PT, labs): document need for continued treatment (e.g., progress, goals, functional status).
- When a policy has specific criteria (e.g., number of visits, specific diagnoses), state in the appeal how the patient meets those criteria and reference the policy by name or number.
